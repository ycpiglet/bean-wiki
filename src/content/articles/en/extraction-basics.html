Concentration and Extraction Yield Are Different Values

Concentration is the percentage of dissolved solids from coffee in the finished beverage. Using the same beans, you can make stronger coffee by using less water.

Extraction yield is the percentage of coffee bean mass that dissolved in water and transferred into the beverage. Because strength alone cannot tell us whether the beans have been sufficiently extracted, we must examine both values together.

The industry commonly references an extraction yield of approximately 18–22% and a concentration (TDS) of about 1.15–1.45% for filter coffee as a balanced starting point. However, this range is not an absolute standard—it is a reference line to be adjusted according to the beans, roast level, and personal preference.

Key Variables That Drive Extraction

As grind size becomes finer, the surface area in contact with water increases, and flow resistance also increases. Generally, extraction accelerates, but variables that harm uniformity—such as channeling and fines migration—can also increase.

  • Grind size and particle distribution
  • Ratio of coffee to water
  • Water temperature and mineral composition
  • Contact time and agitation
  • Uniformity of water flow through the coffee bed

Change One Variable at a Time

When adjusting a recipe, it is easiest to reproduce results by first setting your target beverage amount and ratio, then matching the time using grind size. Recording the flavor and changing only one variable at a time allows you to compare cause and effect.

Do not conclude over- or under-extraction based on a single word like sourness or bitterness. It is important to evaluate clarity of aroma, sweetness, mouthfeel, aftertaste, and overall balance together.
