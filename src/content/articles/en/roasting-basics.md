## Roasting progresses through continuous stages {#stages}

When heat is applied to green beans, moisture first evaporates (drying), followed by deepening color and flavor formation (browning), and as the bean expands, the flavor profile develops. This process flows continuously without interruption, and we gauge progress through several signals along the way.

- Yellow point: When considerable moisture has evaporated and the bean takes on a yellow hue
- First crack: A sound caused by internal pressure expanding and rupturing the bean, marking the start of development
- Second crack: Appears at higher levels of roasting; from this point onward, surface oils and dark roast characteristics intensify

## Color is an indicator of roast level {#color}

Bean color is the most intuitive clue for judging roast level. Since the surface color and the color of ground powder can differ, it's important to observe both, and when possible, compare them against standard color references.

However, color is only a summary of the result, so even identical colors can yield different flavors depending on batch and roast profile. It's important not to determine quality or flavor characteristics based on color alone.

## Reading color, aroma, sound, and time together {#reading}

Generally, light roasts tend to emphasize acidity and the origin's inherent characteristics, while dark roasts highlight bitterness, body, and roasting-derived aromas. Yet this is merely a tendency—it varies considerably depending on the green bean and roast profile.

Therefore, rather than relying on color alone, the shortcut to understanding roasting is to record aroma, crack sounds, time, and temperature together, and finally verify the results through cupping.
