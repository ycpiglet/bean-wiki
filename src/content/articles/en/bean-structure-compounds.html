Green Beans Are Made of Tightly Packed Cells

Green beans are composed of tissue woven with sturdy cell walls. Inside and around these cells are compounds such as sugars, organic acids, lipids, proteins, and caffeine. Roasting and extraction are processes that extract these compounds and transform them.

Key Compounds That Create Flavor

Each compound plays a different role in flavor. Below are representative examples, though actual proportions vary depending on variety, cultivation, and processing.

  • Polysaccharides and cellulose: Structural components that form cell walls and are mostly insoluble in water.
  • Sugars: The foundation of sweetness and important precursors for the browning reaction during roasting.
  • Organic acids: Determine the impression of acidity and change as roasting progresses.
  • Lipids (oils): Trap aromas and contribute to texture and body.
  • Caffeine: Contributes partly to bitterness and is relatively heat-stable.

Compounds Transform into Flavor at Each Stage

During roasting, numerous aromatic compounds are newly created through the Maillard reaction—where sugars and amino acids react—and through caramelization of sugars. At this point, the bean's color and aroma change dramatically.

During extraction, compounds soluble in water move into the beverage. Therefore, even with the same beans, the final flavor differs depending on how the roasting and extraction are performed.
